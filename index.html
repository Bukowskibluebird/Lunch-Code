Of Iron and Innocent

poem by Ryan Hayes

I come home every few months.
The town changes, little by little,
like an old relative.
A new perfume, a different dress,
another tumor.

Like a thief, time robs me
while I sleep.
It's the little things that vanish first.
They're gone, as if they had never existed,
as if I'd made it all up.
Even now, I feel like I'm asleep,
like I'm not really here.
Like I'm not really anywhere.

The places we used to go—
they long for us in the wind,
wondering where we've gone
and if we'll ever return.

I will return to them,
once or twice in this lifetime,
never knowing which moment
will be the last.
It won't ever be the same.

We've let you die.
I'm sorry.

Like the fading,
whispering jazz halls,
you never knew how much
you loved her until she's gone
or beyond recognizable.
Or maybe it's me she can't recognize.

I come home every few months,
and I climb into the bed of my boyhood.
I sleep on the edge;
there’s no more room for any more
than there ever was.

After all,
I'm not really here,
am I?
There's a shadow pushing
me out, a little more each night.
I am not welcome here.

I try to remember
what it was like to be that child,
that shadow, that stranger
in my old skin.
Strange how alive he is,
how a corpse smiles.

I want to ask him why left,
but the words don’t form.
I won't.
I can't.

I can't wake that beautiful boy,
that face my mother loved so.
I can't risk it.
What if he breaks again?

That is to say,
Do you ever feel more like
a place than a person?
Like a vacancy waiting to be filled?
People come,
only to go.
No, they don't complete you;
they're not supposed to.
But what good are you alone?

In this flesh, I wait,
a way station stranded in this
rat race of the living,
wings clipped, heart bolted and
shackled to the wastes of the earth.

I am no different than
all the places I have abandoned.

Behind a chain-link fence,
I watch their backs
fade into the distance.

I was born to watch you leave me.
